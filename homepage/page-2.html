reading #1

Jenny Oddell’s “How to Internet” article was very interesting and captivating to read. Looking at the Internet through a historical perspective was very unique as it feels like both such an old and new invention. There were two parts in Oddell’s article that especially intrigued me. First, is the transition to “the sponsored how-to”. As Oddell said, “what got people excited about the internet originally was how it allowed relatively unmediated access to regular people”. Now, a lot of the content we now see online is heavily mediated and sponsored. I was also intrigued by the Hole in Space installations in Los Angeles and New York back in 1980. Seeing the awe people had to this technology back then makes me realize that our ability to stay connected to people no matter the distance today is something that we take for granted.

I enjoyed reading Laurel Schwulst’s article as well, especially with the various metaphors to describe the roles and functions of a website. A website is such a vague concept to me that seeing it through metaphors was definitely easier to comprehend. Though as I read the entire article, I was grounded by the sentence that states “Today more than ever, we need individuals rather than corporations to guide the web’s future.” Even the creator of the World Wide Web also realizes that capitalism’s hold on the Internet is threatening its ability to perform the most good. This power over the Internet must return back to individuals.

Questions:

  • How are individuals going to be able to regain control over the Internet from corporations?
  • How can content creators balance continuing to produce sponsored content to gain more profit, while not disrupting the watching experience for the viewers?
  • Do you believe artists should create their own space on the web? Why or why not?

    • reading #2

    Laura Baladi’s essay described how archiving was used in the 2011 Egyptian Revolution as an act of Resistance. Photography, filming, social media posts, and art displays later on were seen as acts of rebellion as they captured a significant fight against injustice, archiving history as it unfolded. In addition to describing how archiving was used during this year, Baladi also provided commentary on why archiving a revolution is important. Parts of the essay that especially stood out were how watching back clips of film agitated locals and showed them the need for solidarity and how artists are reclaiming public production of their art to reflect their revolutionary beliefs.

    I thoroughly enjoyed Paul Soulellis’ talk at Eyeo 2019. His thoughtful elaboration on how to resist normative design practices was especially interesting as someone who is passionate about the intersection of art and activism. Being introduced to strategies such as radical publishing, queering design, failure, and collective care was eye opening and comforting as it showed me a new perspective to designing. Other aspects of his talk that stood out to me was when he posed the questions “What is urgent? And urgent for whom?” and the notion that not everyone is able to participate in “good trouble” equally. I definitely will be following urgentcraft and QUEER.ARCHIVE.WORK’s work!

    Questions:

  • What differentiates something simply labeled as political art from art as a form of activism?
  • The 2011 Egyptian Revolution is called the “Facebook Revolution”. Does social media’s positive impact on politics/revolution outweigh the negative?
  • How can artists be more mindful of resisting capitalist strategies when designing and producing?

    reading #4

    Often, discussions regarding social issues are avoided in technology spaces. I appreciated reading Black Gooey Universe by American Artist because of how it seamlessly intersected these topics. Silicon Valley is widely known as an “incubator for the research and production of high technology”. Although to this day, Silicon Valley and the tech industry as a whole is still run by straight cis white men. This is especially alarming as technology once reserved for the rich and powerful are now more widely available to the masses. How is the tech industry supposed to create and develop technology to be used by a diverse group when those that call the shots do not share these diverse perspectives? Inevitably, the technology they will create will be discriminatory – as seen in the anti-blackness present in how GUI allowed the switch from black to white screens.

    Rich User Experience, UX and Desktopization of War also intersected the two issues. This article was the first time I heard the concept of “Desktopization of War”, but it made sense completely as the way that technology has made war more accessible to the masses as well as aided war is something I am well aware of and concerned about. The section that discussed the use of armed drones was especially disturbing to me. The gamification of war through films, video games, and new technology such as this has led to a higher societal acceptance of the inhumanity that occurs during wars. Though highly concerning, I doubt that any change will occur due to the hold of capitalism on the war and tech industries and how intertwined these two industries are.

    Questions:

  • What other “technological advancements” showed the lack of diversity in the tech industry?
  • Will the gamification of war in the tech industry always persist or do you see a path to change?
  • What positive changes has the introduction of UX brought to the tech industry?

    • reading #5

    Surveillance and Identity has always intrigued me. I always lightheartedly say that the idea of being surveilled concerns me much less than most people I know, because I do not have anything particularly to hide. However, consuming the media for this week’s reading made me realize how naïve of a perspective that was.

    Looking in Dewey-Hagborg’s project, I realized that surveillance required so little – even just a strand of hair or a chewed up gum I left behind. I also once thought that surveillance only allowed you to look in and inspect one’s identity. Although as Dewey-Hagborg created strangers’ faces through 3D printing simply from a trace of DNA, the gravity of surveillance seems larger and more dangerous.

    In Harry’s article, I was also able to get a broader view of what surveillance is – never really linking it to policing and other abusive dynamics. Understanding that surveillance is “based on a presumption of entitlement to access, by right or by force” allowed me to understand how the most marginalized in society are most affected by it. This realization made me see the privilege I had to not fear surveillance daily as a non-Black person of color and also as a documented immigrant.

    Questions:

  • Alongside the technological advancements of the past decade came increased surveillance of consumers. Why is this seemingly increase of invasion of privacy seen as an advancement?
  • Being aware of the especially oppressive history and present state between surveillance and the Black community, how do we responsibly consume media that broadcasts their trauma? Should we avoid consuming such media?
  • In regards to Dewey-Hagborg’s project, does she cross an ethical line in her 3D printing creations despite the criticism of surveillance inherent in their work?

    • reading #6

    An Xiao Mina’s talk on Memes to Movements was very intriguing. Memes are considered to be more frivolous media items, but this makes them more accessible to the masses. Memes have the ability to make complex topics more digestible for anyone to understand no matter their socioeconomic status and/or education level. I definitely agree with how memes can become large parts of movements. Although, as the use of memes for social justice issues become more normalized, the easier it will be to censor – which is saddening as this used to be a strength of being able to use memes.

    For example, in my home county of the Philippines, the current government criminalizes any act of dissent – tagging any activists as terrorists. With the Philippines being a country that is very active across all social media platforms, the government has realized that some activists may use social media as their main method of mobilization and advocacy. Therefore, they are aware to be on the lookout for these activists online and one might risk their life by posting political memes.

    Questions:

  • How have memes made activism and social justice work more accessible to the masses?
  • How does poor image build alliances and create global networks?
  • Glitch art is described to be “disturbing, provoking and horrifying… beautifully dangerous”. What aspects of this style allow it to convey such an image?

    • reading #7

    This was the first time I read about Afro-now-ism from Stephanie Dinkins and it was especially enlightening. The notion of having direct input from the public to better “infuse AI ecosystems with nuanced ideas, values and beliefs toward the equitable distribution of resources and mutually beneficial systems of governance” was something I never thought of before but made complete sense. The tech industry seemingly thrives off of barriers of access, so this made me optimistic about its future.

    Throughout the past years, I have read about examples of bias in machine learning – or the “coded gaze” as Joy Buolamwini named it. From her talk, I especially appreciated her call of action in the end that encouraged listeners to believe and practice the three tenets of “who codes matters”, “how we code matters”, and “why we code matters”. Placing these statements as central to one’s coding instead of an afterthought is essential for progress.

    Mimi Onuoha’s *69, Pathways, and Missing Datasets projects were all so fascinating. The central message of her talk was captured when she stated that “conversations about data and collection are actually just conversations around power and control”. I was also fascinated by the notion that considering the collection process itself is also as important as considering the data itself.

    Questions:

  • How would AI ecosystems benefit from gaining direct input from the public?
  • What examples of bias in machine learning have you experienced or read about?
  • What is the impact of seeing the value of the data collection process?

    These readings were incredibly fascinating. The intersection of technology and activism has always intrigued me because of technology’s proven large scale influence on society – both positively and negatively. The Cyberfeminism Index was fascinating. Seeing the long history of activism examples in this space was something that I was not aware of, but was excited to see.

    Ari Melenciano’s talk “Omni-Specialized Design for Beautiful Futures” was very informative. Hearing about her amazing work with AFROTECTOPIA, the “The Revolution Will Be Digitized” course, and Building a Museum 353 Years Into The Future was empowering. The biggest takeaways were the idea of “Design with, not for” and the sustainable concept of circular design.

    Reading the article interviewing Hamid Khan from the Stop LAPD Spying Coalition was agitating. We have been exposed to similar issues of power imbalance and discrimination through surveillance. I admire the coalition’s work - all that they have accomplished and the causes they are continuing to fight for. Their call for abolition is the only way. What I learned from this article about person-based and location-based predictive policing definitely showed the inherent harm and discrimination of surveillance.

    Questions:

  • What purpose do archives such as the Cyberfeminism Index fulfill?
  • How do we implement the idea of “Design with, not for” with our work in class?
  • As technology continues to advance, how do we ensure that discriminatory/harmful technology does not advance alongside that?
